FOREWORD

The Panamanian nation has a vocation of service and connectivity that has marked the history of the country since its inception. With a strategic geographical position, an exuberant natural heritage and robust economic growth results, the country has the necessary elements to guarantee a decent life to its inhabitants, but this will not be possible if the challenge of climate change is not assumed with the most high priority and perspective of the State as a key condition of the environment that puts at risk the natural and material heritage that sustains us.

In compliance with the commitments acquired before the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), the Republic of Panama presents in December 2020, the update of the First Nationally Determined Contribution (CDN1) presented in 2016. This present Update represents the evolution of a climate action perspective from environmental management, towards a management aimed at decarbonizing the economy and increasing climate resilience at the national, local and sectoral levels. This paradigm shift is the goal to which we are committed.

The transformational aspiration of the economy is the main characteristic of the process of updating the climate commitments that we have undertaken as a country, and which, far from representing a point of arrival, is another milestone on the road to sustainable development, low in emissions, resilient and inclusive. Hence the slogan "Panama Transform yourself" as a call for joint and strategic climate action.

The Updated CDN1 undertakes climate action from an integrated approach, which seeks to overcome the adaptation-mitigation dichotomy, proposing commitments that integrate both dimensions. The Updated CDN1 includes the development and implementation of climate planning and management instruments that mark the path towards resilience, covering ten sectors and strategic areas of the Panamanian economy, marking a transition from existing national policy instruments to the implementation and use of operational climate instruments at the sectoral and transversal level, covering the entire economy.

In this sense, together with the commitment to reduce emissions in the energy sector, framed in the context of the Energy Transition Agenda, Panama presents for the first time commitments that include the development and implementation of climate planning and management instruments that they mark the path towards adaptation and resilience, through the elaboration and implementation of six sector plans and four sector guides on climate change.

With the implementation of these sectoral instruments, we seek that, in the medium term, every program, project or undertaking, public or private, integrates the climate as an intrinsic variable from the earliest stages of its idea, design and planning. For this, it joins the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) as a key agent of change in climate action, together with the Ministries and governing institutions that have assumed sectoral commitments.

The convergence of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), the Paris Agreement, the new global framework for biodiversity and recovery from the COVID 19 crisis, offers the opportunity for, placing sustainability and climate action at the center of management economic and social, we will overcome the barriers of poverty and inequality, which, together with the climate threat, constitute urgent challenges for the government and Panamanian society as a whole.

With the commitments presented by Panama in the Updated CRC1, we heed the call of the Paris Agreement to increase the level of ambition. Meeting this challenge requires progress in innovative forms of regional and global collaboration that allow us to accelerate the climate response, maximizing access to and efficiency of public climate finance, and creating conditions to mobilize private climate investment on a large scale.

Milciades Concepción, Minister of the Environment

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Republic of Panama is a diverse and multicultural country, distinguished by a dynamic of economic growth supported by a sophisticated offer of logistics and financial services. The country remains firm in the strategic decision to commit to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and the United Nations Framework Convention on climate change (UNFCCC). The vulnerability of the country to the effects of climate change, both at the level of its physical structures, and of the ecosystem and anthropogenic services platform that sustains the national economy, requires an increase in the level of climate ambition to guarantee the sustainability and competitiveness of the country in the long term, in a development scenario that to be sustainable has to be resilient to the climate.

Following up on the commitments made by the country in the framework of the Paris Agreement (PA), the update of the first Nationally Determined Contribution of Panama (CDN1) is innovative, fair and ambitious, since it is based on a integrated, in which actions for adaptation and mitigation are complemented to build resilience and move towards carbon neutrality in the country by 2050, generating triple benefits. That is, they produce multiple and tangible results in the areas of economic development, social well-being and environmental sustainability, gender equality, and the empowerment of women, youth, and vulnerable groups, by minimizing the damage caused by the impacts of climate change. and at the same time maximizing the opportunities that climate finance offers.

At the same time, it represents an opportunity to reflect greater climate ambition and solve the multidimensional problems linked to lack of equity, existing social inequalities, gender and intergenerational gaps and poverty that continue to affect the country and limit the sustainability of development. . From this perspective, in Panama development and the reduction of poverty and inequality are central elements of climate adaptation and mitigation, as well as the commitment to guarantee human rights and incorporate gender equality as a transversal axis to promote the development of sustainable climate policies, strategies and actions.

A key aspect of the Updated CDN1 presented by the Republic of Panama in 2020 is the incorporation of a broader concept of climate change and resilience in planning for the development of the country, proposing a transformational process of all economic, social and productive dynamics Panamanian to advance circularity, resilience and progressive reduction of emissions. Following the principle of shared but differentiated responsibilities and complying with the provisions of article 4.4 of the Paris Agreement, the country presents a GHG commitment to contribute to the mitigation of global climate change, by advancing towards an absolute goal of reducing emissions for the economy as a whole, prioritizing the Energy and LULUCF (Forests) sectors, which have the greatest impact on national emissions trends. 1.1 Geographical profile

The territory of the Republic of Panama comprises the land surface, the territorial sea, the underwater continental shelf and the air space between Colombia and Costa Rica in accordance with the boundary treaties signed between Panama and these two States. Panama is located in the northern hemisphere, in the intertropical zone near the Equator. The Republic of Panama is geographically located in the low northern latitudes, that is, between 7 ° 12'07 "and 9 ° 38'46" North latitude and 77 ° 09'24 "and 83 ° 03'07 ' ´ of western longitude.

Panama has a continental and insular territory of 75,517 km2, its shape is similar to a flat capital "S" and presents a direction from east to west, contrary to the direction of the rest of the Central American countries, which are presented from north to south. It is arranged in a west-east direction, limits to the North with the Caribbean Sea, to the South with the Pacific Ocean, to the East with Colombia and to the West with Costa Rica. 70% of the national territory is occupied by the lowlands and hills of less than 700 meters above sea level and is made up of the extensive plains of Chiriquí, Veraguas, the Azuero peninsula, Coclé and the coastal plains of the Caribbean. The remaining 30% corresponds to lands above 700 meters above sea level, which include the central mountain range with elevations among which the Barú volcano stands out, which has the highest elevation in the country (3, 475 masl), Cerro Fábrega (3,375 masl) and Cerro Echandi (3,163 masl), towards the West; towards the East, the eastern arc of the North that includes the Sierra Llorona de Portobelo, the San Blas mountain range and the Tacarcuna mountain range and the eastern arch of the South that includes the Serranía del Sapo, the Serranía de Majé and the Serranía de Pirre ( MyAMBIENTE, 2018).

The territory is divided into ten provinces (Bocas del Toro, Coclé, Colón, Chiriquí, Darién, Herrera, Los Santos, Panama, West Panama and Veraguas), 81 districts or municipalities, 679 townships (two districts with a corregimiento level: Kuna de Madugandi and Kuna de Wargandi and three indigenous comarcas with province category: Guna Yala, Emberá-Wounaan and Ngäbe-Buglé) (INEC, 2019).

1.2 Climate

The Panamanian territory presents a uniform tropical climate throughout the year with a seasonality determined by the precipitation patterns marking the Panamanian climate between dry and rainy seasons. Average annual temperature values ​​range between 23 and 27 ° C, with maximums of up to 33 ° C for coastal areas, and at higher elevations the values ​​can drop to 16 ° C. The temperature in Panama is mainly defined by the topography of the territory where it increases about 0.56 ° C for every 100 m of altitude. (TCNCC, 2018). On the other hand, rainfall varies between 1,500 and 3,000 mm per year, with variations according to the topography and oceanic influence; on the Caribbean side, rainfall is higher than on the Pacific side. In Panama, the rainfall regime greatly influences productive activities, the location of forests, biodiversity, among others. Three rainfall regions are identified (Pacific, Central and Atlantic), each with its own characteristics.

The great oceanic masses of the Atlantic and the Pacific are responsible for the high humidity content of the environment, and due to the narrowness of the strip that separates both oceans, the Panamanian climate has a great maritime influence. Regarding the meteorology, the semi-permanent anticyclone of the North Atlantic significantly affects the climatic conditions of our country, since from this system the northeast trade winds are generated that in the lower layers of the atmosphere reach our country, sensibly determining the climate of the country (ETESA, 2009).

According to the National Climate Change Strategy 2050, the main effects associated with climate change include risks from sea level rise and hydro-meteorological events. Regarding the rise in sea level, current critical situations are already facing vulnerable indigenous populations. A critical case is the situation of the Guna Yala Region, an archipelago located on the Caribbean coast of the country, for which since 2014 the need to plan displacement processes from the archipelago to the continental zone has been documented (Displacement, 2014). Coastal risk modeling tools suggest flood scenarios in 2050 that compromise critical areas of the canal operation in Panama City.

1.3 Socio-economic characteristics

According to data from the Strategic Government Plan (PEG) 2019-2024, the Panamanian economy has developed as a service economy, and that is where its comparative advantage lies. The transit center constitutes 83% of GDP, 90% of service exports, and 65% of the population. The logistics activity is constituted by the transshipment, storage, consolidation, repackaging, assembly and redistribution in ports, Colon Free Zone, Panama Pacifico, City of Knowledge and export processing zones (PEG 2019). This vocation of transit and connectivity positions the country as an access platform to the Latin America and the Caribbean (LA&C) region.

Since 1990, Panama has had an exceptional economic performance. It has consistently led the region in economic growth rate, with 5.9% on average per year. At the same time, Panama has undergone a structural transformation of its economy, in which the construction, financial and logistics services sectors, as well as retail and wholesale trade have made the greatest contribution to growth. The agricultural sector, one of the sectors with the highest percentage of employment, with 14.4% of all jobs, has constantly fallen in its contribution to GDP, going from 6.2% in 2005 to 3.1% in 2015 and between 2016 and 2019 it has been kept at 2%. 2.1 Process of updating the CRC1, principles and guiding approaches

To update the CDN1, including the design, establishment of commitments and their socialization, a broad, systematic and multisectoral consultation and validation process was carried out, led by the MiAMBIENTE. Each commitment was discussed and agreed with the sector institution responsible for its implementation. In addition to the institutional work sessions for the design, definition and adjustment of commitments, nine interactive virtual workshops for active listening were held with the private sector, public sector, local governments, non-governmental organizations, the scientific community and young people, in which they participated. more than three hundred people, with evidence of participation of at least 53% women and 47% men. Prior to its formalization,

The process of updating the CDN1 resulted in the establishment of twenty-nine (29) structured commitments around ten (10) sectors and strategic areas prioritized as follows:

  • Energy
  • Woods
  • Integrated Watershed Management
  • Marine-Coastal System
  • Biodiversity
  • Sustainable Agriculture, Livestock and Aquaculture
  • Resilient Human Settlements
  • Public health
  • Sustainable Infrastructure
  • Circular Economy

Also including two (2) commitments to strengthen capacities for climate transparency, in terms of determining losses and damages, and monitoring and evaluating climate risks and low-emission development in Panama.

The principles and methodological approaches that guided the updating of the CDN1 are:

  1. Integrated adaptation-mitigation approach: refers to the need to demonstrate how each of the ten prioritized sectors and strategic areas can reduce emissions, while promoting synergies and potential benefits in resilience of populations and improvement of livelihoods, generates Green jobs and triggers improvement of results in health, education, and reduction of inequality.

  2. Participatory and gender-sensitive planning and decision-making approach for resilient and inclusive development: refers to the definition of contributions and the mechanisms for their implementation are agreed with national, sectoral and local actors, ensuring broad participation and distributing burdens and opportunities, in the most equitable and fair way possible, while recognizing gender as an essential issue and taking into account norms, gender roles and inequalities as part of its objectives.

  3. Nature-based solutions and green infrastructure approach: refers to nature-based solutions (NBS) that include those actions that integrate ecosystems and the services they provide, to respond to various societal challenges such as climate change , food security or disaster risk. The “green infrastructure” constitutes a good alternative or complement to the conventional engineering approaches, known “hard” engineering or “gray” infrastructure.

4.1 Energy Sector

In Panama, the Energy sector takes into account emissions from stationary combustion and mobile sources from the burning of various types of fuels. It is the largest GHG emitter, according to the greenhouse gas inventory data (INGEI) of Panama (MiAMBIENTE, 2020).

4.1.1 Sectorial circumstances

The Republic of Panama is a country with an economy based on trade and service. In 2019, national energy consumption by sector was distributed as follows: 47.4% represented transportation, 19% industrial, 17.1% commercial and public services, 16% residential, and 0.5% others (SNE, 2019). To satisfy this demand, the national energy supply came mainly from imported sources in 82.3% (oil derivatives, coal and natural gas) and from natural resources in 17.7% (hydro-energy, wind, solar, biogas, bagasse and firewood), according to the 2019 energy balance. It is evident that Panama has a diversified energy matrix, however, it still depends considerably on the import of fossil fuels and, therefore, is subject to the volatility of world prices.

Of the total energy supply (38,918 Kboe), 48.7% was directed to electricity generation. The installed capacity by type of source in 2019 was 43.9% (hydroelectric), 24.9% (oil derivatives), 10.3% (coal), 9.3% (natural gas) and 11.6% (non-conventional renewable energies, distributed in 6.6% wind, 4.8% solar, 0.2% biogas). It is important to highlight that these numbers are close (77% of the goal to 2030) to the commitment presented in 2016 in the CDN1 of Panama on 15% of Non-Conventional Renewable Energies (NCRE) to 2030.

Taking 2014 as the base year where the participation of ERNC was 2.1%, in 2019 an increase in participation of ERNC of 9.5% is reflected. It is important to highlight that multiple measures of direct impact have been carried out to achieve this increase in NCRE, among them a series of fiscal incentives for the promotion of hydroelectric generation that include mini-plants, wind, solar and natural gas plants. , as well as the modification of the energy and power purchase rules. Additionally, there are energy efficiency measures such as the definition of fourteen (14) energy efficiency indices, current technical standards and regulations for air conditioning and refrigeration, with their respective energy labeling, current technical regulations for sustainable building,

However, the Energy sector still has a series of challenges that it must overcome, including: 1) closing the energy access gap in the country, 2) reducing the consumption of fossil fuels and making greater use of the potential of renewable energies the country has, 3) attend to the quality of electricity service, which has been affected by the growth of the population and its concentration in Panama City, San Miguelito, Arraiján, David and Colón, 4) reduce the electricity and hydrocarbon subsidies, maintaining electricity and fuel prices acceptable to the end user, 5) mitigating greenhouse gas emissions from the transportation sector, which in 2017 corresponded to approximately 57% of the sector's emissions,6) adapt the energy sector to the adverse effects of climate change such as increases in temperature and changes in precipitation patterns, which have the potential to impact the demand, distribution, transmission and generation of energy.

4.2 Forest Sector

This sector includes activities on Land Use, Land Use Change and Forestry, known by its acronym LULUCF. This sector is important for mitigation and adaptation to climate change. In Panama, in terms of mitigating climate change, the sector acts as a net sink for GHG and gross emissions indicate deforestation as the main source, especially the transition from natural forest to pasture. In addition, the sector has the potential to contribute to the reduction of vulnerability and the increase of climate resilience through adequate forest management and increase of forest cover in hydrographic basins, which generates a series of ecosystem services that benefit the most vulnerable communities and protect their livelihoods.

4.2.1 Sectorial circumstances

The LULUCF sector is strategic given that the Republic of Panama has 4,925,789.72 hectares of forests, which represent 65.40% of the national territory, which is why Panama belongs to the group of countries with the highest percentage of forest cover in the world, as indicated by the National Forest Strategy 2018-2050. 4.3 integrated estion of river basins

Under this strategic area, reference is made to the management of water resources, which constitutes one of the main engines of economic and human development in Panama as it is the pillar of the country's economy, agriculture and energy security.

4.3.1 Sectorial circumstances

Panama is considered one of the countries with the highest water resources, with approximately 35,894 m3 of renewable freshwater resources per capita (GWP 2017). This abundance scenario is relative, since it hides a series of regional and seasonal limitations, specifically those associated with the area known as “Arco Seco”, the most arid region of the country. The scenario of relative abundance of current and future water coexists with serious conflicts due to competition for the use of water that tend to worsen, in quantity and intensity. In turn, this scenario of relative abundance of water and growing conflicts is aggravated by climate variability and extreme weather events, mainly droughts and floods.

According to statistical and meteorological records, since 2004 there has been an increase in the frequency of extreme events in the country, with hydro-meteorological events having affected the most different ecosystems, as well as the most vulnerable population, including women. boys and girls, people with disabilities, people in poverty and the elderly, in several priority watersheds at the national level. The crises resulting from extreme precipitation events that have occurred in recent years, including the events of La Purísima 2010, El Niño 2015, and the storm Otto 2016 and the storm ETA in 2020, have highlighted the vulnerability of the systems around the collection. , distribution and access to water.

Evidence of the dependence of the national economy on water resources is the operation of the Panama Canal, an energy generation matrix made up of 60.1% hydroelectricity and agriculture highly dependent on hydrological cycles, with little coverage of irrigation systems. By supporting logistics, transportation and financial services, water management is key to the socio-economic and environmental functioning of the country. Complementary sectors such as energy and tourism are also directly related to water management, for the use of resources and for ecosystem services (water supply, scenic beauty, recreational uses, others). From this perspective, the management of water resources is the basis of the economic, social and environmental sustainability of the country.

Water management in Panama is carried out based on an integrated water and basin management approach. The water-food-energy-climate nexus is widely recognized and has been applied in Panama to advance in a resilient management of water resources through the Program of Adaptation to Climate Change through Integrated Management of Water Resources in Panama in implementation since 2018 with resources from the Adaptation Fund. Despite the fact that the legal framework for water is outdated in terms of climate action, the sector has a long-term strategic instrument in implementation that can be the framework for said transformational adaptation towards a national model of water resilience, the National Plan for Water Security (PNSH) 2015-2050: Water for All (PNSH),

In addition to the legal gap, the resilience of the water sector faces other weaknesses related to the lack of a consolidated meteorological network aimed at generating a database for the study of climate change with a record of data disaggregated by sex that evidences the different vulnerabilities to which exposed by men and women, and lack of budget for the execution of research programs and planning of water resources at the level of hydrographic basins.

Regarding gender, the National Water Security Plan (PNSH) 2015-2050: Water for all, highlights that water scarcity exacerbates gender inequality, and in some cases causes child labor in girls. For this reason, Panama is committed to achieving equitable access to adequate sanitation and hygiene services for all people, paying special attention to the needs of women and girls and people in vulnerable situations. To this end, the government will facilitate the participation of men, women, children and vulnerable groups in the management of the sustainable use of water resources at all levels and in the distribution of benefits, and a gender perspective will be integrated into the design and implementation of water resources management programs.

4.4 Marine-Coastal Systems

This sector refers to the strategic management of marine and coastal resources that include the waters of the territorial sea, estuaries, submarine continental shelf, coastlines, bays, estuaries, mangroves, reefs, underwater vegetation, scenic beauties, biotic and abiotic resources within said waters and the coastal strip.

4.4.1 Sectorial circumstances

Panama has an extensive coastal zone with a length of 1,700.6 km in the Pacific and 1,287.7 km. In the Caribbean. The most common climate threats reported to the coastal zone are sea level rise, storm surges, cyclonic storms, strong winds, floods, droughts, landslides and earthquakes. Additionally, these threats also increase with the concurrence of the extreme effects temporarily produced by the El Niño weather phenomena. The effects on the infrastructures of fishing communities, events of saline intrusion and coastal erosion, among other damages, associated with weather events require the incorporation of a disaster risk reduction approach in the management of marine-coastal systems. Although there is insufficient data on the level of impact of climate change on marine-coastal ecosystems, there are important indications. For example, data from the San Cristóbal tide gauge station in Panama indicate a trend of sea level rise on a scale of increase of 1.4 mm per year (Biomarc-USAID, 2013). On the other hand, the affectation of coral reefs due to increased temperatures reported in areas of the Western Pacific of Panama has been documented through biodiversity monitoring programs.

Coastal zone management is carried out by applying gender-responsive integrated coastal management policies and standards, in order to improve the quality of life of communities that depend on coastal resources through the management of coastal and sea spaces, as well as through of resource management actions. Among the instruments used for coastal management, the existence of five special coastal-marine resource management zones and at least forty-six marine protected areas stand out, which have been established and managed regardless of climatic considerations.

In Panama, isolated initiatives have been implemented to incorporate climate into coastal management, among which the following stand out: i) the Project for the protection of carbon stocks in mangroves and protected areas3; ii) the initiative “Blue Economy (BE): Caribbean Large Marine Ecosystem Plus (CLME +)”: Promoting national priorities of the blue economy through marine spatial planning in CLME + 4; iii) the National Action Plan for Marine Litter5; iv) Participatory Diagnosis on the effects on the Garachiné community's livelihoods associated with coastal erosion and preparation of a Community Strategy and Action Plan6; v) Participatory Diagnosis on the effects on the livelihoods of the Garachiné community associated with coastal erosion and preparation of a Community Strategy and Action Plan7. While these are ambitious initiatives, a general coastal climate resilience framework is needed to facilitate coherence and complementarity between present and future initiatives. Additionally, they face other limitations related to a low level of involvement of coastal communities, land tenure problems in the coastal strip, as well as insufficient geospatial data and a sustained increase in anthropogenic pressures on fragile coastal marine ecosystems, especially mangroves and peatlands.

The loss of coverage of wetland ecosystems, mainly mangroves, increases the levels of vulnerability of the coastal zone. In 2018, the National Wetlands Policy was approved through Decree 127 of 2018 aimed at avoiding new losses and applying restoration measures, including among its action guidelines promoting the generation of opportunities and strengthening the capacities of community-based organizations to take advantage of business opportunities and other economic alternatives offered by the sustainable use of wetlands with gender equity.

The National Action Plan for Sustainable Fishing recognizes the fundamental role that women have in the fishing sector, as fisherwomen, workers in processing plants, traders, preparers of inputs for fishing, activists and representatives of fishing groups / communities. In addition, national data show that the livelihoods of women who depend on marine-coastal resources will suffer disproportionate impacts caused by the degradation of the oceans, loss of marine biodiversity and natural resources. For these reasons, it is essential that the regulations and strategies related to the regulation of marine-coastal systems and their sustainable use are gender responsive,

4.5 Biodiversity

Biodiversity refers to the variability of living organisms from any source, including, among others, terrestrial and marine ecosystems, found within each species, between species and between ecosystems.

4.5.1 National circumstances

The richness of Panama's biodiversity is extensively documented, with significant figures in contrast to the size of the territory. The National Biodiversity Strategy 2018-2050 highlights that, in part, due to its location as a biogeographic bridge between the flora and fauna of Central and South America, Panama is home to approximately 3.4% of the species of amphibians of the world, 2.3% of its reptile species, 9% of known bird species, and 4.8% of mammal species. In Panama, a total of 220 species of freshwater fish and 1,157 species of marine fish have been identified. It is also among the top 25 countries in the world in terms of diversity of flowering plant species. There are 10,444 plant species known in the territory (3.3% of the world's diversity). Of this, 9,520 are vascular, of which 17 are gymnosperm species and 938 species of ferns and allies; Of the 924 non-vascular, 796 are species of mosses and nearby groups (MiAMBIENTE, 2018a).

Climate change is one of the top five pressures driving the loss of biodiversity in the world, along with habitat loss, overexploitation, pollution, and invasive alien species. Panama is developing initiatives to conserve ecosystems, habitats and natural landscapes in situ, with a focus on restoring protected areas and biological corridors to guarantee interconnectivity.

As for evidence on the level of affectation and vulnerability of biodiversity associated with climate change and variability, there are partial data. For example, there is historical data on the impact of reef ecosystems as a result of monitoring programs mainly in the Western Pacific of Panama, but there is no similar information for the rest of the country or in relation to other ecosystems. The ecosystem-based adaptation approach has been applied in specific pilot-type projects, such as the project for the protection of carbon stocks in mangroves and protected areas in Panama.

The National Biodiversity Strategy and Plan 2018-2050 highlights the different perspective that men and women can have on biodiversity, its intrinsic value from the environmental point of view and its social value, as an element that contributes to human life. It is also recognized that the actions of conservation, sustainable use and restoration of diversity can be a dynamic element in local rural communities in the country, particularly benefiting young people and women.

Compliance with international commitments on biodiversity acquired under the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), the Strategic Plan for Biodiversity 2011-2020 (known as the Aichi Targets) and the process for the new framework Biodiversity Global Framework towards the 2050 Vision of “Living in Harmony with Nature”, among other global frameworks, is carried out in Panama in the context of a broad strategic policy framework for the management of biodiversity given by the following main instruments:

4.7 Resilient human settlements

4.7.1 Sectoral circumstances

4.9 Sustainable infrastructure

This strategic area refers to the need to design infrastructure that adjusts to local contexts, provides efficient services, and is durable, addressing environmental risks and sustainability, by ensuring financial resources to build and maintain the infrastructure during its useful life. , consider the preferences and needs of the population, and understand the political and institutional dynamics to ensure that projects survive political cycles (IDB, Sustainable Cities).

4.9.1 Sectorial circumstances

In recent years, construction activity in Panama has been oriented mainly to residential homes, offices, commercial premises and tourist infrastructure. The importance of this sector in economic dynamics lies in the multiplier effect it exerts on the demand for goods and services from other sectors of the economy and in its great capacity to employ unskilled labor. However, the construction model of the country has been based on a traditional vision of gray engineering that has resulted in an accelerated urbanization process not planned environmentally or climatic, with important territorial asymmetries.

In addition, the construction standards applied show limited knowledge of the risk and vulnerability to climate change by the governing entities in the development of public infrastructure and private developers. There are tools to promote the sustainability of infrastructures at the urban level and there are initiatives to apply voluntary eco-efficiency standards that are mainly aimed at energy efficiency. These initiatives still need to be scaled for greater application and reach at the national level.

Making the nexus between natural and built infrastructure visible is a key aspect in moving towards a sustainable infrastructure model. The loss of ecosystem protection services due to the impact on coastal wetland ecosystems increases the vulnerability of the coastal zone, while the increased risk of disaster due to land use conversion and poor construction practices affects both the urban area as rural of the country. The cyclical dynamics of loss of public road infrastructure, as well as housing and livelihoods due to floods and extreme weather events is evidence of the need to transform construction models and practices to incorporate disaster risk and climate variables.

4.10 Circular Economy

The circular economy proposes a new social economic model through which resources, material flows, energy and waste are used and optimized. The transition to a circular economy implies acting transversally at all levels and sectors of the national economy, influencing lifestyles and individual consumption patterns, up to the transformation of industrial processes and the incorporation of sustainability into development agendas. local, at the level of municipalities and other instances of local development.

4.10.1 Sectorial circumstances

In general terms, Panama continues to work on the basis of a linear economy that, although it generates profits and jobs for the country in its position as a logistics and multimodal transportation center, demands more and more resources (water, energy and food), through while generating more organic and inorganic waste and less capacity for ecosystems to regenerate over time. Waste management in the country is still incipient, lacking both knowledge and investment resources to stimulate the required sector transformation.

The experience accumulated in both the public and private sectors in implementing cleaner production initiatives since 2007 has been key to advancing the recent development of an enabling legal framework to stimulate the transformation agenda towards a Circular Economy that includes the following regulations :

  • Law 33 of 2018 establishes the zero waste policy and its framework of action for the integral management of waste and mandates the sectoral entities to promote the import, manufacture and commercialization of products that favor the integral management of waste, as well as regulate the import of materials or products whose valorization or integral management is limited or non-existent in the country;
  • Law 1 of 19 of 2018 that prohibits the use of single-use polyethylene bags in retail establishments, expanding the restriction for warehouses and wholesalers;
  • Law of February 6, 2018 that establishes the integrated management of solid waste in public institutions.
  • Municipal Agreement 124 of 2015 of the Municipal Council of Panama through which the Municipal Policy for Comprehensive Solid Waste Management and Zero Waste Program 2015-2035 of Panama City is adopted.

In October 2019, an Agreement was signed between the MiAMBIENTE, the Industrial Union of Panama (SIP) and the National Council of Private Enterprise (CONEP) for the creation of the Circular Economy Center of Panama.

Waste management actions also have repercussions on gender equality, considering that initiatives by women's groups focused on the reuse and recycling of materials for the production and sale of various products have been documented in the sector. Improving the situation of the sector presents the opportunity to improve the situation of women, many of whom are involved in waste management informally. Actions to promote a circular economy through the integral management of waste and solid waste in a sustainable way has the potential to promote sources of decent employment that promote gender equality and economic empowerment of women.

The Ministry of the Environment (MiAMBIENTE) as the national focal point for climate finance facilities, together with the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MINRE) are the main responsible for this strategic climate alignment process for mobilization of global and regional resources.

The Republic of Panama actively participates in cooperative initiatives to advance climate action, ranging from instances to influence the international climate negotiation process, to thematic programs and access to climate financing windows. Some of these international spaces for climate action in which Panama participates at the time of the CDN1 update are:

  • Programa EUROCLIMA+
  • Coalition of Nations with Tropical Forests
  • NDC Partnership
  • Partnership for Market Readiness
  • Association of Greenhouse Gas Programs of Latin America and the Caribbean
  • Ibero-American Network of Climate Change Offices
  • Latin American Network of National Greenhouse Gas Inventories
  • Independent Association of Latin America and the Caribbean
  • Global Covenant of Mayors for Climate and Energy.
  • Pacific Rim Ocean-Climate Action Partnership

Likewise, the Republic of Panama maintains collaborative work with agencies of the United Nations system such as UNDP, UN Environment, FAO, and international and multilateral financial institutions such as IDB, CABEI, World Bank, CAF, to access sources of climate finance, including the Adaptation Fund, the Green Climate Fund and the Global Environment Facility (GEF), among others.

Even with these cooperation supports, the challenges for a coherent implementation of this Updated CRC1 are multiple, including without being restricted to:

  • Prioritization of the climate dimension in the post-2020 economic reactivation
  • Climate finance strategy as part of foreign policy, fiscal policy and public credit
  • Strengthening of climate negotiation and relationship capacities at all levels
  • Operationalization of inter- and intra-institutional implementation mechanisms and arrangements for budget planning and execution in a climate-sensitive manner
  • Structuring transformational climate programs successfully presented to global and regional financial institutions to increase available financial and knowledge resources
  • Overcome the perception that the climate issue is the sole or priority responsibility of the environmental authority, clarifying the roles and responsibility of each of the actors and sectors in the framework of climate policy and action.
  • Unlock private climate investment, reducing dependence on public financing
  • Use the information generated by the monitoring and evaluation system for decision-making beyond the Ministry of the Environment and the purely public sphere

The synergy, coherence and complementarity between the sources and processes of climate finance will be the guiding principle of the relationship with cooperation agencies and development banks and the private sector. In light of the provisions of the Enhanced Transparency Framework (MTR) of the Paris Agreement, the achievement of coherence and complementarity between the different financing opportunities will be facilitated through the implementation of the National Platform for Climate Transparency (PNTC) previously described.

In operational terms, the kick-off of the Updated CDN1 deployment will be directly supported by a set of means and deployment arrangements that includes but is not limited to:

  • National Adaptation Planning Process (NAP) that Panama will carry out with the support of the preparatory program of the Green Climate Fund (GCF), with the United Nations Environment Program (UN Environment) as implementing partner
  • Preparation of the National Climate Action Plan (PNAC) with the support of the World Bank with emphasis on the investment plan for the implementation of the CDN1 Updated
  • Process of implementation, monitoring and updating of the CRC led by the MiAMBIENTE with the intervention of the National Committee on Climate Change of Panama (CONACCP), as has been regulated through the provisions of Chapter II on the CRC of Executive Decree 100 of 2020 that regulates the mitigation of climate change and establishes other provisions.
  • Collaborative agreement with private sector unions to promote the circular economy and sustainable finance
  • Article 12 of the Paris Agreement recognizes that improving climate change education, as well as training, awareness and participation, are key elements to improve global action on climate change, as they promote changes in lifestyles , attitudes and behaviors that promote low-emission, resilient and sustainable development.
  • The Action for Climate Empowerment (known as ACE for its acronym in English) is a transversal proposal in public policies that seeks to generate and articulate capacities that promote transformative action against climate change, relying on education, training, awareness social, access to information, citizen participation and international cooperation, as essential elements for the implementation of training processes and political and social strengthening on climate competencies. For this, common work between all sectors, areas and institutions and at all scales (from global to local) with citizens is necessary.
  • The implementation of the Updated CDN1 begins with the implementation in 2021 of capacity building processes for climate action that include:
  • Joint program between the Ministry of the Environment and the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) for training in the structuring of public investment projects incorporating the climate dimension. The objective is to establish the bases and standards so that by 2025 all the projects that enter the National Public Investment System (SINIP) duly integrate the climate dimension. In parallel with the support of the CAF, the capacity building of the consultants involved in the preparation of environmental impact studies and environmental audits will be supported, in light of the updated regulations on environmental management instruments based on the conceptual framework of change. climate and climate variability analysis.
  • Strengthening the capacities of the Panamanian financial sector to create an enabling environment for climate finance, through initiatives for the involvement of the private sector and the design of innovative financial mechanisms, in strategic association with the UNEP FI initiative and the Development Bank Club ( IDFC), taking advantage of resources from the Green Climate Fund preparatory program.
  • The entry into implementation in 2021 of the Regional Agreement on access to information, public participation and access to justice in environmental matters in Latin America and the Caribbean, known as the Escazú Agreement, ratified by Panama through the Law N ° 125 of 2020, which is a concrete opportunity to address and mitigate the climate crisis through a rights-based approach, recognizing the nexus between climate change, poverty and inequality. The operationalization of this nexus is the basis of the call for climate action in Panama embodied in the Updated CDN1, to follow the path towards the decarbonization of the Panamanian economy and the model of sustainable, resilient and inclusive development for present and future generations of Panamanians and Panamanians.

Geography and climate

The territory of the Republic of Panama comprises the land surface, the territorial sea, the underwater continental shelf and the air space between Colombia and Costa Rica in accordance with the boundary treaties signed between Panama and these two States. Panama is characterized by a very hot tropical climate, which prevails throughout the year on the coasts and lowlands, but which changes inland as it gains altitude (INEC, 2005).

Water resources

Panama is considered one of the countries in the world with a great water resource, this water wealth is generated by a hydrographic network made up of 52 basins, which collect the waters of some 500 rivers of two slopes, the Caribbean and the Pacific. This water regime is conditioned by the position, orientation and relief of the Isthmus (INEC, 2005).

Forest Cover

In 2019, the Ministry of the Environment through the Directorate of Environmental Information (DIAM) presented a diagnostic map which detailed the state of forests and other wooded lands. According to the report generated, Panama has 65.4% of forests and other wooded lands, 32.5% of other lands and 2.1 of continental waters (MiAMBIENTE, 2019a).

Forests in Panama are of great value thanks to the multiple services and benefits they provide us, however, it is necessary that they be monitored since a lot of pressure is exerted on them due to forestry and agricultural activities.

Demography

Estimates of the total population made for the year 2018, establish a figure of 4,158,783 inhabitants, which in relation to the total area of ​​75,517 km2 in Panama, represents a population density of 55.07 inhabitants / km2 (MINSA, 2019a).

Economic Profile

Before the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic and, Panama was among the fastest growing economies in the world, with an annual average of 4.6% in the last five years. The country has made progress in reducing poverty in recent years, partly due to economic growth and public transfers. Between 2015 and 2018, it would result in a decrease in poverty from 15.4% to 12.6%, while extreme poverty declined from 6.7% to 5.2%. Panama is considered one of the most unequal in the region (World Bank, 2020)

Impacts, risks and vulnerabilities of the country

As already mentioned, Panama is a highly vulnerable country to the impacts of climate variability and change, although compared to the countries of the Central American and Caribbean region, it is in a privileged condition.

With respect to climate variability, during the period 1990 to 2013, in the Republic of Panama a total of 2,717 events of natural origin were registered; Of these, 57% correspond to floods, 17% to gales or strong winds, 15% to landslides, while the remaining 11% is distributed in other events. It should be noted that, within the mentioned climatic phenomena, floods are the ones that have caused the greatest number of people affected and deaths in the study period.

However, the records or reports of damage have tended to increase and are closely related to the behavior of extreme events registered in neighboring areas in the last decade. The foregoing allows us to infer that, although the population may be better prepared, the effects on infrastructure, communication routes or public services may be susceptible to damage and with collateral impacts for the population.

The IPCC Fifth Assessment Report (AR5) states that since 1950 many changes have been observed in extreme weather events (IPCC, 2014). Panama is no exception. The changes shown in climate variability in recent years have increased by around 1-2 ° C, which has highlighted a condition of increasing vulnerability in key economic sectors: water resources, agriculture, human health, marine areas- coastal and resilient cities.

Despite the abundance of water resources in Panama, the crises resulting from extreme precipitation events that occurred in recent years such as La Purísima 2010, El Niño 2015, and storm Otto 2016, and the most recent impacts from tropical storms ETA and Iota 2020, as well as the rainfall deficits observed during El Niño in 2015, where the levels of water sources at the national level were critical. All these events have highlighted the vulnerability of the systems around the collection, distribution and access to water.

With regard to the marine-coastal areas, it is known that these resources have been seriously threatened by the pressure exerted by human activities and the impacts associated with climate change. Among the most common climate threats in these areas are strong winds, floods, droughts, landslides, earthquakes, rising sea levels, and saline intrusion. Against this background, the vulnerability of coastal settlers and economic sectors is threatened, particularly in the face of changes in climate variability that, year after year, seem to intensify the extreme values ​​of rainfall and temperature. One of the economic activities on which the effects of global warming will be observed the most is agriculture, livestock and aquaculture. They stand out among the main effects: the modification in crops, due to the increase in the atmosphere in the concentration of carbon dioxide; greater probability of an increase in the pest population, and adjustments in the demand and supply of water for irrigation.
